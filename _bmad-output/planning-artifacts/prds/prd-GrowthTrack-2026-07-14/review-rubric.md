# PRD Quality Review — GrowthTrack Phase 1 (WhatsApp Sales Reporting & Admin Portal)

## Overall verdict

This PRD has real substance: a disciplined, unusually consistent use of `[ASSUMPTION]`/`[NOTE FOR PM]`/confirmed-vs-inferred tagging, an honest Non-Goals section, a coherent thesis (meet field staff on WhatsApp rather than build a portal habit) that its Success Metrics actually validate, and a shape (capability spec + minimal UJs) well-matched to a single-portal-role internal tool. It is undercut by one systemic mechanical defect — every "§8"/"§9" inline cross-reference in the first half of the document points to the wrong section (Cross-Cutting NFRs / Constraints instead of Open Questions / Assumptions Index), 12 times — plus two concrete internal inconsistencies worth resolving before sign-off: an unqualified "500+ concurrent users" NFR that sits awkwardly against the PRD's own Administrator-only portal framing, and a Manager persona that appears in the JTBD list but never drives a distinct requirement. None of these are fatal; all are fixable without re-architecting the PRD's structure.

## Decision-readiness — strong

The PRD states decisions as decisions rather than burying them as considerations, and it names what's given up, not just what's chosen. §4.1's `[ASSUMPTION: this resolves a tension in the source SPEC between CAP-1... ]` (line 92) is explicit about the RBAC tension it's resolving, and addendum §A5 spells out that the RBAC constraint "still holds at the data-model level... it just has a narrower present-day surface" — an honest trade-off, not a smoothed-over one. §5 Non-Goals goes further: the `[NOTE FOR PM]` on Managers losing portal access says plainly "this may be emotionally load-bearing for Managers who want direct visibility... worth revisiting if timeline or stakeholder pressure permits" — a real objection acknowledged, not dodged. §10 Data Governance does the same for PDPA data residency: flagged as a legal-review item that "does not block PRD, architecture, or build work," but also names the downstream consequence honestly ("if legal review requires localization, it will likely reshape the production WhatsApp provider decision").

Open Questions (§13) are genuinely open — e.g. OQ1 (source-of-truth system) and OQ2 (Recipient Group/Channel modeling) are flagged as blocking architecture, not rhetorical placeholders with an answer already implied. `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts land at real tensions (FR-2's future multi-role authorization, FR-4's brand thresholds needing "a business decision, not an engineering guess") rather than safe checkpoints. No findings.

## Substance over theater — adequate

**Strong:** NFRs in §8 carry actual numbers (3s dashboard load, 60s generation, 99.5% uptime) rather than "must be scalable/secure" boilerplate. The Vision (§1) is specific to this product — WhatsApp-as-primary-channel, Bangladesh pharma/FMCG, Phase 2 sequencing logic — not swappable boilerplate. Differentiation claims are appropriately hedged and pushed to the addendum (§A1: "No product was found that uses WhatsApp as the *primary* channel... this is a genuine differentiator," sourced to a dated research sweep) rather than oversold in the main narrative.

**Weaker:** the Manager persona doesn't earn its place. §2.1 JTBD lists "As a Manager, I want visibility into team-wide achievement and growth trends... so I can coach reps proactively," but no UJ features a Manager, and no FR specifies how a Manager's Daily Report differs from a Sales Rep's — particularly for a Manager overseeing multiple Sales Teams. The Glossary (§3) papers over this with "Manager... Receives WhatsApp content like a Sales User," which answers the channel question but not the content-scope question the JTBD itself raises.

### Findings
- **medium** Manager JTBD doesn't drive a distinct requirement (§2.1, §3, FR-6) — The Manager JTBD asks for "team-wide achievement and growth trends," but FR-6's report content spec (§4.5, "team performance, top/focus brand, doctor list") is written from a single-rep/single-territory point of view, and no UJ or FR consequence addresses what a Manager overseeing multiple Sales Teams actually receives (one team's data, all teams aggregated, or a Sales-Rep-shaped report that doesn't fit their JTBD at all). *Fix:* either add a UJ/FR consequence for the Manager report shape, or explicitly note in §4.5 that Manager content is out of scope for Phase 1 differentiation and flag it as a `[NON-GOAL for MVP]`.
- **high** "500+ concurrent users" NFR contradicts the PRD's own single-portal-role framing (§8, §12) — §2.2 and §4.1 are explicit that "only Administrators authenticate to the web portal" in Phase 1 (a small population), yet §8 Cross-Cutting NFRs and §12 Operational Requirements both state "system supports 500+ concurrent users" without saying concurrent *what*. Read as portal sessions, it's implausible given the described user population; read as WhatsApp-recipient dispatch concurrency (which would make sense given FR-6's 5-minute delivery window), it's plausible but never said. This reads as SPEC-inherited boilerplate not reconciled to the role model this PRD itself establishes. *Fix:* name the population the bound applies to (WhatsApp send fan-out vs. portal sessions) and adjust the number if it's the latter.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The thesis is explicit and load-bearing: "deliver that visibility automatically, every day, through the channel field staff already use — WhatsApp — instead of asking them to adopt a new app" (§1). Feature prioritization follows from it — the portal stays a thin Administrator control plane (§4.1, §4.7, §4.8) while the WhatsApp delivery mechanism (§4.5–4.6) carries the product's actual value, matching the Vision's framing. Phase 2 (AI forecasting) is deferred with a stated dependency argument ("Phase 1's data pipeline and delivery infrastructure existing and working first... not a consolation scope cut," §1) rather than a vague "later."

Success Metrics validate the thesis rather than measuring activity for its own sake: SM-1 (delivery success rate) and SM-3 (duplicate-send rate, target 0) measure the reliability the whole value proposition depends on; SM-4 (manual notification adoption) checks whether the escape hatch is actually used, not just built. Counter-metrics are genuinely counterbalancing, not decorative — SM-C1 explicitly forbids tuning retry logic to hide failures just to make SM-1 look better, and SM-C2 forbids dropping Dashboard fields to hit the SM-2 load-time target. No DAU/MAU-style vanity metric is present.

### Findings
- **low** No explicit sequencing or de-scope priority among the 8 in-scope features (§6.1) — All eight features are listed as co-equal MVP scope with no signal of what would be cut or deferred first under timeline pressure, despite this being a real-launch PRD where that pressure is plausible. *Fix:* not required, but a one-line prioritization note (e.g., "delivery + directory management are the floor; Brand Analytics and Doctor Prioritization are next-most-cuttable") would help a PM under schedule pressure act on this PRD directly.

## Done-ness clarity — adequate

Every FR has a "Consequences (testable)" block with concrete, checkable conditions — HTTPS-only, exact timing windows (60s generation, 5-minute delivery, 3-second dashboard load), exactly-once delivery, append-only audit log, etc. There is no instance of "handles gracefully" or "reasonable performance" anywhere in the document (verified by search) — a genuine strength most PRDs don't achieve.

What keeps this from "strong": one NFR is untestable as written (see the "500+ concurrent users" finding above, which belongs here as much as under Substance over theater), one FR consequence is underspecified, and a cluster of FRs have a load-bearing parameter deferred to an Open Question — honestly flagged, but still not closeable by an engineer without an external decision first.

### Findings
- **medium** FR-8's "attach a report" is undefined (§4.6, FR-8; also UJ-1) — FR-8 says the Administrator can "optionally attach a report" to a Manual Notification, and UJ-1 says Rehana "optionally attaches the current performance report," but no consequence specifies what that attachment actually is — a Dashboard snapshot, the Daily Report format, a PDF, an image? None of FR-8's testable consequences cover this. *Fix:* add a consequence naming the attachment's format/source, or mark it `[open question]` alongside the others in §13 if genuinely undecided.
- **low** A cluster of FRs have a "done" state gated on unresolved Open Questions — FR-1's session TTL, FR-4's brand-tier thresholds, FR-7's retry attempt count/backoff, and FR-12's audit retention period are each honestly marked `[open question]`/`[NOTE FOR PM]` rather than hidden, which mitigates the severity — but it means four of twelve FRs cannot be fully verified as "done" until those items resolve. *Fix:* none needed beyond resolving §13 OQ3/OQ9/OQ11/OQ12 before the affected stories are built (already flagged there).

## Scope honesty — strong

§5 Non-Goals is substantive, not a token section: six bullets, each with a stated reason (AI forecasting deferred to `roadmap-phase2.md` with a dependency argument; Manager/Sales-User portal access deferred with the emotional-cost `[NOTE FOR PM]` noted above; Twilio-as-POC-only with the production decision explicitly preserved in the addendum rather than silently dropped). The PRD is also unusually careful to distinguish *confirmed* decisions from *inferred* assumptions inline — e.g. FR-9's "User accounts are created manually... *(Confirmed with the user during PRD discovery; a decision, not an inferred assumption.)*" versus FR-10's "`[ASSUMPTION: added to satisfy WhatsApp's business-messaging opt-in policy...]`" — which is exactly the kind of rigor that prevents an architect from over-trusting a guess.

Open-items density is high for a real-launch PRD — 12 Open Questions (§13), 8 Assumptions Index entries covering 10 inline tags, and 4 `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts — but the PRD partially earns this by prose-flagging the two that actually block architecture (OQ1: "the highest-priority item to resolve before architecture work locks in an integration approach"; OQ2: "both need a modeling decision before architecture proceeds"). What's missing is a structural way to see that triage at a glance.

### Findings
- **medium** Open Questions aren't structurally triaged by urgency (§13) — The list mixes items that block architecture (OQ1, OQ2), a pre-launch legal gate (OQ5), and lower-urgency parameters (OQ9 retention, OQ10 support model, OQ11 TTL, OQ12 retry policy) in one flat numbered list. The urgency signal exists only in prose modifiers buried in parentheticals, which a downstream reader (or a PM triaging a backlog) has to re-derive by reading each item closely. *Fix:* group §13 into "Blocks architecture," "Blocks production launch," and "Resolve during build," or add a priority tag per item.

## Downstream usability — adequate

The Glossary (§3) is thorough (18 terms) and FR/UJ cross-references mostly use IDs rather than "see above" — e.g. FR-8 says "Recipient selection reuses the Recipient directory (FR-9)" rather than pointing vaguely upward. FR, UJ, and SM identifiers are contiguous and unique (FR-1–FR-12, UJ-1–UJ-3, SM-1–SM-5 plus SM-C1/C2, no gaps or duplicates), and every UJ has a named protagonist carrying context inline (Rehana, Farhan) — no floating UJs.

The dimension is held back by a systemic, repeated broken-cross-reference defect: every inline "(§8)" and "(§9)" pointer in the document is wrong. §8 is actually "Cross-Cutting NFRs," not Open Questions; §9 is actually "Constraints and Guardrails," not the Assumptions Index. This looks like a section-renumbering artifact — five sections (§8 Cross-Cutting NFRs, §9 Constraints, §10 Data Governance, §11 Integration, §12 Operational Requirements) appear to have been inserted ahead of what were originally §8 Open Questions / §9 Assumptions Index (now §13/§14), and the inline pointers were never updated. See Mechanical notes for the full list.

### Findings
- **high** Systemic broken cross-references to Open Questions and Assumptions Index (12 instances) — Every inline reference intended to point at Open Questions (§13) instead reads "(§8)," and every reference intended to point at the Assumptions Index (§14) instead reads "(§9)." This starts in the very first substantive paragraph of the document (§0, line 13: "inferred details are tagged `[ASSUMPTION]` inline and indexed in §9" — actually §14) and recurs through the Glossary, FR-1, FR-3, FR-6, FR-7, FR-12, §5, §10, and §12. Full list in Mechanical notes. *Fix:* global find/replace of inline "§8" → "§13" and "§9" → "§14" in reference contexts (not the section-8/section-9 headings themselves), then a proofread pass to confirm each corrected reference actually makes sense in context.
- **medium** Assumptions Index has two entries with no matching inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tag (§14) — The "OAuth2 is not required for Phase 1" entry (line 336) has no corresponding `[ASSUMPTION]` marker anywhere in §4.1/FR-1/FR-2 — a reader relying on inline tags to find assumptions would never encounter it. Similarly, the "Currency formatting is Cr BDT" entry (line 340) isn't tagged where currency is actually stated, in FR-3 (line 126) and FR-6 (line 167) — both present Cr BDT as settled fact with no bracket marker, so a reader scanning FR-3/FR-6 for `[ASSUMPTION]` tags would treat currency format as confirmed when the PRD itself considers it inferred. *Fix:* add inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags at FR-3/FR-6 for currency, and at FR-1/FR-2 (or §4.1) for the OAuth2 non-requirement, so the roundtrip holds in both directions.

## Shape fit — strong

This is correctly shaped as a capability spec for a single-portal-role internal tool, not forced into a heavier template. Each of the 8 features (§4.1–4.8) reads as a capability with testable FR consequences, matching the "internal tool, single-operator role" guidance rather than padding with UX-heavy structure the product doesn't need. UJ usage is well-calibrated rather than decorative: of the 3 UJs, 2 (UJ-1, UJ-3) illustrate the Administrator's two real portal workflows (manual send, delivery-gap investigation), and the third (UJ-2) illustrates the WhatsApp recipient experience that is the product's actual value delivery mechanism — not portal-UJ padding for a single-operator tool, but targeted coverage of both sides of the product. Success Metrics are appropriately operational (delivery success rate, duplicate-send rate, audit completeness) rather than consumer-engagement metrics that wouldn't fit a WhatsApp-push, no-login product. No findings.
